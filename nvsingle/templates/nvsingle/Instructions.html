Instructions for Part II

In this part of the study you represent a firm that sells products from an inventory to customers. Your only task is to make one decision: how many units to order into your inventory. Below we explain how you make your decision and how the profits from this part are determined.

Order and demand. Your order of units to the inventory can be any whole number between 1 and 100. However, you can only sell the amount of units that is demanded by the customers who visit your inventory. If you do not have enough units to satisfy all demand, some customers will leave your inventory empty handed. On the other hand, if you have too many units in your inventory, some of them will be leftovers.

Demand is randomly drawn as a number between 1 and 100. Each value of demand has an equal probability of occurring. You will know the demand only after you have made the decision on how much to order to the inventory.

Revenue. Your revenue will be determined by how many units you are able to sell to your customers. If the demand is higher than your inventory level, then your revenue is determined only by the amount that is in your inventory. If the demand is lower than your inventory level, then your revenue is determined only by the amount that is demanded. The customers will pay 4 pence per each unit that is sold from the inventory.

For example, if your inventory level is 40 units, but your demand is 60 customers, then the extra 20 customers will leave your inventory with empty hands. In this case your revenue is 4 × 40 = 160 p. If the demand turns out to be 30 customers instead of 60, your revenue is 4 × 30 = 120 p, and you have 10 units in the inventory as leftovers.

Ordering cost. You have to pay 2 pence for each unit that you order to the inventory. Note that due to the randomness of demand, it may be that your ordering cost will be higher than your revenue, resulting into losses.

Profit. Your profit is calculated by subtracting ordering cost from revenue. Measured in pence (£ 1/100), profit is therefore:

    If demand is less than ordered units: 4 × [demand] − 2 × [ordered units]
    If demand is more than ordered units: 4 × [ordered units] − 2 × [ordered units]

If lossess occur, these will be subtracted from your baseline reward.

When you make your decision, you are provided a profit calculator that you can use to calculate the profit for any combination of ordered units and demand.

Monetary reward. You will be paid the average of the two profits that you get from this part and Part III, where you will be making a decision in a similar situation. For example, if your profit in this part is 90 pence and in Part III, 120 pence, your monetary reward is (0.90 + 1.20)/2 = £1.05. This will be added to your reward from Part I.
