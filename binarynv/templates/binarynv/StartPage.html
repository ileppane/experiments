You are a firm maintaining an inventory. You will be making decisions over many rounds. On each round, your task is to decide how large to make your inventory.

Your inventory system is depicted by a square that encloses two circles, a blue circle and a red circle.

You set the size of the blue circle to determine inventory size. This is the number of products that you stock, hence the bigger the blue circle, the more products you stock.

The size of demand is represented by the red circle. This is the number of products that people want to buy from you. The size of the red circle will be determined randomly after you have set the blue circle. Thus, you will only know the demand after you have set your inventory level.

At your decision page, there are two options at which you can set your inventory level:

  • The quantity that maximises your expected profit (OPT)
  • The quantity that is equal to the mean demand (MEAN)

Both inventory size and demand are values between 1 and 300. You can only sell what is demanded.

If your inventory is not large enough to satisfy all demand, some customers will leave your inventory empty handed. If your inventory is larger than demand, you will have leftover products which do not bring you any profit.

Each value of demand has an equal probability of occurring. Each random draw of demand is independent of previous draws.

Your revenue will be determined by how much of your inventory you are able to sell to your customers. If the demand circle is larger than your inventory circle, then your revenue is determined only by your inventory circle. If the demand circle is smaller than your inventory circle, then your revenue is determined only by the size of the demand circle. Numerically, the customers will pay you 12 pence per each unit that is sold from the inventory.

Note that due to the randomness of demand, it may be that your ordering cost will be higher than your revenue, resulting into losses.

Your profit is revenue minus cost. You will be able to use a profit calculator between the decision rounds to see how revenues, costs and profits behave when you adjust the inventory size and the random demand.
